Chart 5 — Does Salary Track with Inclusion? Sector Drives Pay, Not Quota Compliance · NIRF 2025

Average salary (LPA) by institution type for UG 3-Year and UG 4-Year programmes, shown alongside EWS% and SC+ST+OBC% enrolment. The key finding: salary differences reflect the sector and discipline of the institution, not its level of inclusion. High-salary public engineering institutions (IITs, NITs) also happen to meet quotas — but the salary is driven by engineering placements, not by the reservation percentage.

✅ High salary + High inclusion (IIT & NIT) IITs pay ₹17.9 LPA (UG4) with SC+ST+OBC near 50%. NITs pay ₹12.9 LPA also at quota compliance. These institutions disprove the idea that inclusion lowers salary — they are the highest-paying and the most inclusive.
⚠️ High salary + Below quota (AIIMS UG3) AIIMS has strong salaries (~₹10–12 LPA) but EWS enrolment below 10%. Their salary advantage comes from medical specialisation, not from low inclusion — it is a supply-demand effect in healthcare.
📚 Low salary + High inclusion (Central Universities) Central Universities (SC+ST+OBC ~52–54%) report lower average salaries (₹8–8.2 LPA) because most graduates go into Higher Education (68–82%), not direct employment. The salary figure covers only the small fraction who took jobs — it is not representative of outcomes.
❌ Low salary + Low inclusion (Private Minority) Private Minority institutions have both low salaries (~₹5 LPA) and low inclusion (~11% SC+ST+OBC). But the low salary is driven by the general arts/management sector they mostly operate in — not caused by low reservation compliance.

Key caveat: Salary values are type-level proxies from NIRF 2025 summary data, averaged across institutions of that type that reported data. They represent the median salary among placed students only — institutions with high Higher Education rates (Central Univ, IISER) have a small placement base, making their average less stable.
Salary should not be used as the primary metric for comparing inclusive vs non-inclusive institutions because it is confounded by sector (engineering vs. arts vs. medical).
